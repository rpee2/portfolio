Design of 3D-printed steel

Overview

For a class project, my team designed a 3D-printed steel using thermodynamic modeling software. I modeled the toughness of the steel, while my other teammates worked on the strength and thermal profile of the steel. I applied my thermodynamic knowledge and learnt a bit of how to use Python for data science.

Goal

3D-printing of metals is an emerging field with the promise of using less material and fewer equipment compared to traditional manufacturing. There are many barriers to widespread adoption of 3D-printed metals however, as 3D-printing inherently creates pores in the metal that weaken the metal. The goal of the project for my class was to design a steel that would have the same strength and toughness as a traditionally-manufactured steel.

Design

My team researched the processing techniques of metals. The processing controls the structure of the metal and hence its properties. By designing the steps in the processing of the steel, we could determine the final properties of the steel. We decided to put the steel through additional heat treatment steps after the initial 3D-printing step. Generally, under specific conditions, the first heat treatment step can form the major component of the material known as austenite. The austenite can transform to martensite given the right conditions, which increases its toughness. The second heat treatment step can, under specific conditions as well, form precipitates that increase the strength of the steel. We then created a flowchart to visualize the relationship between the processing to the structure and to the properties of the steel.

Modeling

I took responsibility for the toughness portion of the steel. I used the thermodynamic modeling software MaDe to calculate the stability of the austenite phase at different temperatures and I used Python to calculate the yield stress of the austenite phase at different temperatures by applying a thermodynamic equation. I plotted the two variables on a graph and predicted the temperature at which the highest toughening would occur. My team used the model to iterate different parameters until the temperature came within the operating temperature of the steel, which is assumed to be at room temperature. I used Python to consolidate my steps.

Result

Based on the thermodynamic models, we were able to design a 3D-printed steel that met our criteria. It had a strength of more than 896KPa and toughness of more than 10KJ/m2. One of my main takeaways from this project is a deeper understanding of materials properties and metallurgy in general. I also learnt how to use thermodynamic modeling software and a bit of Python.
